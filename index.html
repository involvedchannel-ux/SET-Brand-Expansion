SET

Brand expansion

Visual identity expansion, secondary element, logo facelift, color update

An addition to the existing brandbook – an expansion of the brand's presence through this secondary brand element. By Koborking studio.

01 What new value are we adding?

The previous identity work produced many sub-brands and extensive guidelines, but it did not create a distinct, unified presence on shelf or in practice. The real-world effect of the guidelines was weak.

Instead of another full rebrand, we are introducing a secondary brand element: a corner/cube shape that carries recognition where the logo alone cannot. This document sets down the rules and approaches for that element, plus a small logo facelift and a refined brand color. Typography and the rest of the existing system stay as they are.

What follows is a concise guide to applying these changes consistently across all touchpoints.

02 Logo Facelift

The logo has received a facelift: subtle internal cuts and spacing adjustments. The essence, structure, and wordmark remain the same.

The goal was to calibrate the mark for all sizes. It now reads well at both very small (labels, icons) and very large (posters, signage). Most customers will not notice the change. No market announcement or redo of existing applications is needed. The changes are noticeable mainly at huge or very small sizes, where alignment and optical balance matter most.

03 The CUBE

A fixed position. The point from which we can move the world.

This is the most important part of our contribution. The logo is a typemark – it spells out "SET."

The cube becomes the secondary element associated with the brand and will always be present in some form. The goal is to build a system that carries the brand's unique character, whatever new situations may arise. New surfaces, formats, or contexts may appear that we cannot foresee today; this system allows us to adapt while staying recognisable.

The core idea

"SET lives along the structural elements." Edge profiles and baseboards are the detail that finishes a space – invisible when done right, but the absence of them leaves a space unfinished. The cube mark indicates the point in a space from which structural elements originate.

Situational approaches

The cube adapts to the situation, so the same concept characterises every application.

When there is a space

– 3D interior renders, photography, real showrooms –

From any viewing angle, we can identify the three planes of a room that meet at a corner. We rotate the mark to match that angle and place the cube at the centre where the three edges meet.

We draw coloured lines (in the brand's red) along the three edges of the three planes. These lines meet at the cube. Even if the cube is small, the highlighted edges draw attention toward it – the composition stays bold and striking.

The cube can be rotated to match any room angle, with the cube placed at the centre where the three edges meet.

Corner treatment applied to 3D interior renders, with highlighted edges drawing attention toward the cube.

The same logic applies to photography, 3D renders, and real showrooms: the space becomes the stage, and the corner-cube becomes the embodiment of the brand.

When there is no space

– Posters, print, big-box retail (e.g. Dedeman), side-by-side with competitors

Here we extract the cube alone. No room lines – a simpler form, but the same idea. The cube becomes its own symbol and carries the heavy lifting.

In a big-box context, competitors typically show an interior (render), lots of text, and a small logo. Our approach: put a gigantic cube in the foreground. It becomes the main attraction. Instant differentiation – bold, striking, and a direct continuation of the corner concept.

The interior can sit in the background or inside the cube as a window. We show the brand boldly. This solves a practical problem: a single baseboard or edge profile is hard to make visually compelling; interiors sell the idea of a finished home, but on their own they are not unique. With this approach we can show a finished interior and still have something distinctive, something striking that (so to speak) jumps off the shelf.

The cube extracted as a standalone symbol, ready to carry the brand when no room context is available.

Hero posters with the cube as the main attraction, creating instant differentiation in a big-box retail context.

These formats work across trade shows, retail displays, and digital campaigns.

When space is limited

– Labels, thin formats (e.g. edge profiles, upside-down labels)

On thin labels for edge profiles, the logo is often doubled (one orientation per edge) so it reads correctly when the product is upside down. The cube appears as a symbol – sometimes before the text – but not huge, because the label must show other information.

The cube can serve as a cutout shape for photos in the middle.

Labels for edge profiles.

The system is designed to scale up or down without losing the idea.

What stays consistent

  • The cube always comes from the same concept: SET living along the structural elements of a space; the detail that finishes the space.
  • The cube should feel engineered and architectural; its primary role is not decorative.
  • New situations will arise. The system is designed to adapt – we apply the same logic to new formats, surfaces, or contexts so the brand's unique mark is always present in some form.

04 Color

We are updating two primary colors from the existing palette: the main red and the main black. The rest of the palette (secondary colors, light blue, white, etc.) remains as defined in the original brand book.

Main red (updated)

Hex#F2004A
RGBR 242, G 0, B 74
CMYKC 0, M 100, Y 70, K 5

Previously: #FF0043 (Pantone 192 C).

A slight magenta shift differentiates the brand from Vodafone's red, which the previous colour closely resembled. The new red keeps the same energy and impact but reads as distinctly our own.

Main black (updated)

Hex#1A2029
RGBR 26, G 32, B 41
CMYKC 37, M 22, Y 0, K 84

Previously: #212128 (Pantone Black 6 C).

Instead of pure black, we use a coloured dark grey – a blueish dark that becomes the brand's own black. On text over white, it reads as black; on larger backgrounds, the blue undertone becomes visible and adds personality. It keeps dry documents (online or print) from feeling flat and gives the brand a subtle but recognisable character even in neutral layouts.

In practice – These slight changes in nuance create a unique pairing. Just the two colours on their own produce a distinct vibrating contrast. It draws on simultaneous contrast (one of the seven core contrast types): enough contrast to read, but not enough to make the background disappear. The effect sits on that borderline and taps into a subtle psychology – when noticed in peripheral vision, it can make people look again. It is another way to draw attention, but a much more subtle one.

05 Typography

The existing typography of the brand remains unchanged. We keep the current typefaces, weights, and hierarchy as defined in the original identity and brand book. This document does not introduce new fonts or a new typographic system; it focuses on the secondary corner/cube element, the logo facelift, and the updated color.
